# Fieldwork Instruments Bank

Instruments used during active fieldwork. Selected per study based on research question type — not a fixed set.

**Framework:** Say / Do / Make / Enact (Sanders & Stappers 2012; IDEO Method Cards 2003)

---

## Suitability check — run before selecting instruments

| Category | Required when | Produces |
|---|---|---|
| **SAY** | Research question involves how participants consciously position themselves: stated values, vocabulary, self-reported experience | Conscious attitudes, participant vocabulary, self-positioning |
| **DO** | Research question requires actual behaviour in context, or temporal/spatial distribution of the phenomenon | Behaviour in situ, environmental affordances, temporal pattern |
| **MAKE** | The phenomenon is pre-verbal, somatic, or identity-adjacent — below easy articulation | Tacit knowledge externalised in non-verbal form |
| **ENACT** | The phenomenon is situational or decision-dependent — occurs only under specific conditions | Edge cases, contextual breakdowns, embodied decision-making |

**Minimum:** one instrument per required category.
**SAY and MAKE are not interchangeable** — SAY reaches what participants can articulate, MAKE reaches what they cannot. If both dimensions are present, both categories are required.
**ENACT instruments produce directional data only.** Always pair with event-contingent capture. Roleplaying and bodystorming run within IDI Phase 3 — not standalone sent instruments.

## SAY instruments

### Values card sort
**Produces:** How participants position themselves relative to the phenomenon — which words they claim as identity, which they reject. Rejected words define the identity boundary the product must not cross.
**Use when:** The research question involves ego-syntonic/dystonic dimensions, or the product's language system depends on knowing which words belong in the participant's register.
**Preferred over alternatives:** When vocabulary space is large and contested — multiple possible framings exist and the team does not know which the population claims.
**Cannot do:** Reveal why a word was placed where it was. Always pair with a voice note explanation or IDI probe.
**Format:** 12–20 words sorted into "feels true / partly true / doesn't fit." Then top three with voice note. Variation: forced ranking, image-word matching.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log (vocabulary) · Quantitative integration (distribution) · Brand System naming and terminology.
**Timing:** Mid-study (Day 4–5 of a 7-day study).

---

### Diary / probe
**Produces:** Self-reported experience over time in the participant's own words. Rhythm of the phenomenon — when it appears, what surrounds it, how participants reflect on it.
**Use when:** The phenomenon is episodic and temporally distributed. When the study period is longer than a single IDI can cover.
**Preferred over alternatives:** When event-contingent capture alone produces thin data and reflective depth is needed.
**Cannot do:** Capture the moment of onset. Always pair with event-contingent capture.
**Format:** Structured prompt sent daily or event-triggered. Written, voice note, or photo with caption. Prompts should invite unexpected responses — not questionnaires.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log · Cultural analysis · Journey map (Stage 1–2 behaviour row).

---

## DO instruments

### Environment walkthrough
**Produces:** Physical and spatial context of the phenomenon — proximity of affordances, ambient conditions, environmental triggers.
**Use when:** The product's use context is a strong design variable. When the team is designing for a context they have not personally inhabited.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Verbal accounts of "I was at my desk" are poor data. A video walk showing the desk, the kitchen twelve steps away, the open-plan office — makes the environment a research object rather than a backdrop.
**Cannot do:** Capture the experience itself. Pair with event-contingent capture for experience data.
**Format:** Participant-led short video walk (2–5 minutes) of spaces where the phenomenon typically occurs. No narration required. Researcher timestamps notable environmental affordances. Note: researcher presence may suppress behaviour in shame-adjacent studies.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log · Journey map (touchpoints row) · Product Specification (accessibility and context constraints).
**Timing:** Day 1–2. Primes the participant to attend to context during subsequent captures.

---

### Behavioral mapping / A-day-in-the-life
**Produces:** Distribution of the phenomenon across a full day — when it appears, in what activities, with what frequency, what precedes and follows it.
**Use when:** Temporal pattern is a product design variable (notification timing, widget display, mode surfacing).
**Preferred over alternatives:** ESM timestamp data shows when; behavioral mapping shows what surrounds the when from the participant's own perspective. Combined they are more robust than either alone.
**Cannot do:** Capture the experience at onset. Always pair with event-contingent capture.
**Format:** Participant-produced timeline or map of the day — what they were doing, when, and when the phenomenon occurred. Drawn, written, or reconstructed in a voice note.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log · Journey map (behaviour row, emotional arc timing) · Quantitative integration (temporal density).

---

### Shadowing / contextual observation
**Produces:** Direct observation of behaviour in context — what the participant does, how they move, what they interact with, what they do not notice.
**Use when:** The gap between stated and actual behaviour is expected to be large. When the phenomenon is habitual, automatic, or shame-adjacent in ways self-report is unlikely to capture accurately.
**Preferred over alternatives:** When the phenomenon involves automatic or pre-conscious behaviour and reconstructed accounts will miss it.
**Cannot do:** Access interior experience. Always pair with interview or voice note for the participant's perspective.
**Format:** Researcher observes participant in natural context. Fly-on-the-wall (minimally interactive) or Guided tour (participant narrates as researcher observes). Consent required.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log · Cultural analysis (behavioural layer) · Journey map (behaviour row, Stage 1).

## MAKE instruments

### Collage
**Produces:** Visual and emotional vocabulary for the experience — images and associations chosen without researcher categories. Metaphors, sensory associations, and emotional register that direct questioning does not reach. The voice note explanation is the primary analytical data.
**Use when:** The experience is aesthetic, sensory, or emotionally textured in ways that language flattens. When prior research has used clinical language and the team needs the participant's own register.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Collage surfaces associations; mapping surfaces structure. Use both when both dimensions are required.
**Cannot do:** Produce temporal or spatial data.
**Format:** Participant assembles 5–8 images capturing the experience from any source. Accompanied by a voice note explaining each choice. No tool specified — participant uses whatever is easiest.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log (B and V flags) · Cultural analysis (affinity clusters) · Brand System (voice attributes — emotional register source).
**Timing:** Day 2.

---

### Attention / experience map
**Produces:** Participant's own structural model of how the phenomenon moves through time. Trigger sequences — what happened before the thing that happened before the phenomenon — not just trigger moments.
**Use when:** The pre-onset phase is analytically important. When understanding what builds to the experience matters as much as the experience itself.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Behavioral mapping shows distribution; attention map shows the participant's own causal model of how the day led to the phenomenon.
**Cannot do:** Capture the experience at onset. Always pair with event-contingent capture.
**Format:** Freehand drawing, digital map, or written description. Prompt: map the flow of attention, not the facts. The form the participant reaches for is data.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log · Journey map (behaviour row, emotional arc) · Quantitative integration (temporal density cross-reference).
**Timing:** Day 4.

---

### Storyboard
**Produces:** Sequential visual representation of how the participant experiences the phenomenon — or how they imagine a resolution. Surfaces the narrative arc of the experience: what a satisfying sequence would look, feel, and sound like from inside.
**Use when:** The experience has a clear narrative arc and the team needs to understand it from the inside before designing for it. Also when testing whether a proposed concept direction resonates with the experience before prototyping.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Storyboarding externalises the arc; interviews reconstruct it verbally. Storyboarding often surfaces structural details (the moment just before onset, the imagined resolution) that verbal accounts skip.
**Cannot do:** Produce real-time behavioural data. The storyboard is a projection — it shows what the participant imagines, not what they do.
**Format:** 4–6 panels drawn or described in sequence. Participant draws key moments, annotates what the character is thinking or feeling, adds a voice note walking through the sequence. No drawing skill required.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log (narrative structure layer) · Journey map (emotional arc, opportunity points) · Strategic Direction (experience principles).
**Timing:** Day 5–6.

---

### Cultural probe
**Produces:** Unexpected, participant-driven data about the texture of daily life in relation to the phenomenon. Probes provoke rather than answer — they generate hypotheses, not conclusions.
**Use when:** At the beginning of a study when the research team does not know what questions to ask. When prior research has produced only surface-level data.
**Preferred over alternatives:** When the phenomenon is poorly understood and structured instruments would presuppose what they are designed to discover.
**Cannot do:** Produce systematic or comparable data. Not appropriate when structured comparison is required.
**Format:** A kit sent before the study: small camera, diary with unusual prompts, postcards to complete, stickers to place on a printed week map. Return rate and completion quality are part of the data.
**Source:** Gaver, Dunne & Pacenti (1999).
**Feeds:** Stage 2 research question refinement · IDI Phase 2 probe selection · QES evidence log (unexpected findings layer).

## ENACT instruments

### Roleplaying
**Produces:** A live simulation of how an experience would unfold. Surfaces the gap between what participants say they would do and what they do when in the moment. Edge cases and contextual breakdowns emerge through enactment.
**Use when:** The interaction moment is difficult to observe directly, or when the team needs to test whether a proposed interaction model holds under real conditions.
**Preferred over alternatives:** When the interaction is contextually sensitive and changes depending on who is present, what the environment affords, and what the participant is already doing.
**Cannot do:** Produce naturalistic data. The enactment is artificial — treat as directional only. Always pair with event-contingent capture.
**Format:** Researcher and participant act out a specific scenario based on a real event-contingent capture. Participant plays themselves. Researcher may play the product, another person, or an environmental variable. Debrief: "what was different from what actually happened?" and "what did you notice that you hadn't said before?"
**Feeds:** IDI Phase 3 (depth probe layer) · Journey map (pain points, contextual breakdowns) · Product Specification user flows (hard constraints, edge cases).
**Timing:** IDI Phase 3 only. Not a standalone sent instrument.

---

### Bodystorming
**Produces:** Embodied insight into how an interaction feels from the inside — what the body does, where it goes, what it reaches for. Effective for interactions involving physical movement, spatial awareness, or somatic response.
**Use when:** The product's interaction involves the body in ways screen-based prototyping cannot simulate. When the experience has a strong somatic component.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Roleplaying is broader; bodystorming focuses on the physical dimension specifically.
**Cannot do:** Produce verbal or conscious-level data. Always debrief immediately after.
**Format:** Team members or participants physically act out a scenario in a space approximating the real context. Researcher notes what the body does spontaneously. Immediate debrief: "what did you notice your body doing that surprised you?"
**Source:** IDEO Method Cards (2003) — Bodystorming under Try.
**Feeds:** QES evidence log (B flag) · Product Specification (interaction states, accessibility constraints).
**Timing:** IDI Phase 3 only.

---

### Experience prototyping
**Produces:** A simulation of a proposed experience — not a product prototype but a prototype of the experience the product is designed to create. Tests whether the intended experiential quality is actually produced under realistic conditions.
**Use when:** The team has a proposed concept direction and needs to test whether it produces the intended experience before committing to interaction design.
**Preferred over alternatives:** Rapid prototype comparison tests interaction decisions; experience prototyping tests the experiential logic upstream of any interaction design.
**Cannot do:** Test usability or specific UX decisions.
**Format:** Simulation of the proposed experience using whatever materials are available — paper, roleplay, physical space, pre-recorded audio, a human standing in for the product. Criterion: does the participant experience the intended quality?
**Source:** Buchenau & Suri (2000).
**Feeds:** Strategic Direction (concept direction validation, experience principles) · Stage 3 rapid prototype comparison (determines which experiential hypothesis to test).

## Adding new instruments
