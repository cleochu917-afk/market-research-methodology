# EISR — Experiential Insight Synthesis Research
## A standardised skill for AI-assisted qualitative discovery market research

**Method classification:** Qualitative social listening synthesis with structured insight generation  
**Research type:** Discovery-phase, direction-setting; pre-quantitative  
**Closest professional analogues:** IDEO field research synthesis · Brand strategy insight generation · Lean customer discovery · Ethnographic synthesis without fieldwork  
**Not the same as:** Quantitative syndicated research (Nielsen, Kantar) · Academic panel studies · UX usability testing  

---

## What this method produces

A complete market research package that moves from human experience → psychological portrait generation → community evidence → competitive positioning → consumer insight statement → product/creative implications → role-specific deliverables. It is designed to make a product direction feel *inevitable* rather than arbitrary, and to package findings so that every team role receives only what is directly actionable for their function.

**Final document structure (canonical sequence):**

```
Part 1 — Research foundation
  Phase 1: Community map (with verbatim community voice)
  Phase 2: Pain points by portrait (with verbatim evidence)
  Phase 3: Failed solutions & product opportunities (with verbatim)
  Phase 4: Competitive landscape map
  Phase 5: Signal scan (TikTok / YouTube / forum cross-validation)
  Phase 6: Language harvest
  Phase 7: Consumer insight statement (observation → insight → implication)
  Phase 8: White space analysis (existing solutions vs. our gap, side by side)
  Phase 9: Analogous inspiration

Part 2 — Role packaging (final section, always last)
  Tab-navigable packages for: PM · Engineer · UX designer · Experience designer ·
  Content designer · UX researcher · Marketing/growth · Data/analytics
  (+ any role specific to the product domain)
```

## The critical innovation: the phenomenological brief

### Why conventional problem statements fail

A conventional market research brief says something like: *"Target: people who struggle with dieting. Problem: they eat too much and can't control cravings."*

This produces generic research. It maps a person to a category (dieter, patient, user) and a problem to a label (overeating, addiction, poor willpower). It tells you what the problem is called, not what it feels like from the inside. The research that follows will cluster around that label — and so will every competitor who has ever read a similar brief.

### What a phenomenological brief does differently

A phenomenological brief describes the *internal experience* of living inside the problem — without pathological framing, without category labels, without pre-diagnosis. It narrates what it feels like to be that person, moment by moment, in their own register.

**The canonical example from this methodology's founding research session:**

> *"when i eat i eat fast because i want food and soon as i stop eating i want to eat again. especially if i stopped eating sweets for a while and eat again i keep eating and eating. I try to slow my pace when eating but i think i need some wholesome distraction and it is hard when i eat alone, watching video while i eat will only propel me to find more to snack on. also, the time from lunch to dinner i would wanna find stuff to eat and it is hard to control myself. when i work and study time i wanna find stuff to munch on, i think i need alternative food related activities that is about food but virtual and dont make me wanna eat more. the most serious case is that sometimes i really crave sth super high calories i can not quench that thirst i think i need wholesome distraction too. I am a person who constantly think about food but if I act upon them I get very fat I need to relocate them somewhere for some activity maybe virtual ones."*

Compare this to: *"I have a food addiction and diet problem."*

The stream-of-consciousness version contains: temporal patterns (fast eating → immediate re-craving), trigger contexts (alone, studying, post-restriction), failed coping attempts (watching video — backfired), a self-generated product concept ("virtual food activities that don't make me wanna eat more"), an emotional register (not shame-based, curious and practical), and the user's own language for their experience. The label-based version contains none of this.

### The mandatory phenomenological entry requirement

**EISR requires this type of input. It cannot be replaced by a problem statement.**

When beginning any EISR engagement, actively request the following from the human:

---

*"Before we start the research, I need something specific from you — and it's different from a conventional brief. I need you to narrate your experience of this problem from the inside, the way it actually feels to live with it. Not what the problem is called. Not what category you fit into. Not even what you want to solve.*

*Write as if you're describing a day — or a specific moment — to someone who has never experienced anything like it. Use your own words, even if they're messy or stream-of-consciousness. Tell me: what happens in your body or mind when this occurs? What time of day? What triggers it? What have you tried? What does 'solving it' feel like in your imagination? What's the gap between what you want and what exists?*

*This isn't therapy. It's data. The more specific and interior, the better. Don't edit yourself toward what sounds reasonable — tell me what it's actually like."*

---

**What to do with the phenomenological brief once received:**

1. Do not summarise it into a problem label. Keep it raw.
2. Identify the distinct psychological *functions* the behaviour serves (continuity, regulation, anticipation, identity performance, sensory stimulation, etc.)
3. These functions become the seeds of the portrait generation step.
4. Extract the person's own language — specific phrases, metaphors, emotional register. These seed the language harvest.
5. Note what existing solutions they've already tried and found wanting — this seeds the failed solutions section.

## Input specification

### Required inputs

**Input 1 — Phenomenological brief (mandatory, cannot be substituted)**
Stream-of-consciousness interior narration of the experience. See above. Length: 100–500 words. No editing required, messy is better. Minimum specificity: must include at least one temporal pattern, one trigger context, and one attempted coping mechanism.

**Input 2 — Conventional problem/community framing (required)**
Who is experiencing this? What domain is it in? Is there a known community or existing terminology? This can be brief — one or two sentences. It anchors the search strategy.

**Input 3 — Research scope clarification (required, asked before research begins)**
One question only: *"Do you want me to research all psychological types equally, or anchor primarily around your own experience and treat others as secondary context?"* This determines portrait weighting and community prioritisation.

**Input 4 — Portrait documents (optional — EISR generates these internally)**
If the human has already developed psychological portraits of their target types, these serve as the primary anchor for the research. If not provided, EISR generates them from the phenomenological brief and community research. See Portrait Generation Protocol below.

### Optional inputs

**Input 5 — Known competitors or analogous products**
Any products the human is already aware of. Saves one research cycle.

**Input 6 — Team role list**
Which roles will receive the final package? Defaults to: PM, Engineer, UX designer, Experience designer, Content designer, UX researcher, Marketing/growth, Data/analytics. Add or remove as appropriate.

**Input 7 — Research depth preference**
Full (all 9 phases + role packaging) or partial (phases specified). Default: full.

## Portrait generation protocol

When no portrait document is provided, EISR generates psychological portraits from the following process:

### What a portrait is

A portrait is not a demographic profile. It is not an age range or income bracket. It is a description of the *psychological function* that a behaviour serves for a person — the interior logic that makes the behaviour make sense from the inside. A good portrait makes a reader say "that's exactly me" not because it describes their demographics but because it describes their inner experience accurately.

### Portrait structure (mandatory fields for each)

```
Portrait name: [descriptive, not clinical — "The Cognitive Grazer" not "Type A"]

Core psychology: One paragraph describing the psychological function the behaviour 
serves. What need does it meet? What would be lost if the behaviour stopped?

Behavioural texture: A bulleted list of observable behaviour patterns. 
Specific, not generalised.

Situational framing: One concrete scenario — a single moment — that illustrates 
the portrait in action. Third person, present tense. Specific enough to be 
recognisable.

Emotional logic: One sentence beginning "The body/mind wants..." that names 
the underlying drive.

Key feature: One sentence naming the thing that distinguishes this portrait 
from all others.
```

### Portrait generation method

Step 1: From the phenomenological brief, identify every distinct psychological function the behaviour serves. List them.

Step 2: From early community research (Phase 1 community scan), identify whether each function has a corresponding community cluster. Adjust and expand the portrait list.

Step 3: For each portrait, ask: does this behaviour pattern make complete sense from the inside — i.e., is it internally coherent even if it looks irrational from the outside? If yes, it is a valid portrait.

Step 4: Generate 5–9 portraits. Fewer than 5 usually indicates under-segmentation. More than 9 usually indicates over-splitting.

Step 5: Name each portrait descriptively. The name should communicate the psychological function, not the demographic category.

**Quality test for a portrait set:** Any person experiencing the target problem should be able to read the full set and identify themselves in at least one portrait — ideally in 2–3 simultaneously. If a portrait set produces "none of these is me" for someone clearly in the target population, it has gaps.

**The phenomenological brief contributor should always map to multiple portraits.** Their brief will typically reveal which portraits are highest-priority for research.

## Research execution protocol (phases)

### Before beginning any search

State to the human: *"I will now execute all research phases. This will take some time. I won't ask for confirmation between phases unless I hit a genuine ambiguity I cannot resolve from available information."*

### Phase 1 — Community map

**Purpose:** Identify where the target population lives online and what their distinctive voice sounds like in each community.

**Method:** Google `site:reddit.com` queries for each portrait type's behaviour pattern. Supplement with TikTok hashtag discovery, forum searches (HealthUnlocked, TeamBlind, relevant niche communities). Do not rely on Reddit's own search — Google site search surfaces better historical depth.

**Output requirements:**
- 10–15 communities minimum
- For each: community name, member count/scale, description of which portrait types are present, and 2–3 verbatim quotes that show how that community's members uniquely frame the problem
- The verbatim quotes are not illustrative — they are evidence. They must be actual quotes from actual posts, not paraphrased.

**Quality gate:** Each community card should feel distinctively different from the others. If two communities have similar quotes, one of them is under-researched.

### Phase 2 — Pain points by portrait

**Purpose:** Ground each portrait in real community evidence. Move from "portrait we theorised" to "portrait confirmed by what people actually say."

**Method:** For each portrait, execute targeted searches that match its specific behavioural texture. Use the portrait's "behavioural texture" list as search terms.

**Output requirements:**
- 3 verbatim quotes per portrait minimum
- Quotes selected for: specificity (not generic), interiority (describes inner experience not just behaviour), and distinctiveness (different from quotes in other portraits)
- Failed solutions: what has this portrait type tried? What did they say about why it failed?
- Unmet need: stated in terms of what the product must do, not in terms of what the user says they want

**Quality gate:** The unmet need statement should be derivable from the evidence — not asserted from theory. If the verbatim quotes don't clearly point to the unmet need, more evidence is required.

### Phase 3 — Failed solutions & opportunities

**Purpose:** Document what has already been tried (by individuals and by products) and establish the evidence base for why those attempts failed. This is the foundation of the gap analysis.

**Method:** Search for community discussions about each solution type. What do people say when they recommend it? What do people say when it fails?

**Output requirements for each failed solution:**
- The solution name and the conventional wisdom around it
- The failure mode (not argued — evidenced with quotes)
- Which portraits it fails and why
- Signal strength rating (1–5 dots based on evidence volume)
- 3–4 verbatim quotes showing both the advice being given AND the experience of failure

**Output requirements for each product opportunity:**
- Name of the opportunity
- Evidence that the need exists (verbatim quotes where people independently discovered a version of this solution)
- Which portraits it serves
- Signal strength rating

### Phase 4 — Competitive landscape map

**Purpose:** Establish where existing products and solutions are positioned — brand, core message, tone, channel, and crucially what white space they leave.

**Format:** One card per competitor. Fields: name, core message (what they say they do), tone (how they say it), primary channels (where they communicate), brand frame (what category they place themselves in), and gap (what they structurally cannot address based on their positioning).

**The white space synthesis:** After mapping all competitors, write one paragraph that names the specific intersection of attributes that no competitor occupies. This is the white space. It should be stated positively (what we can be) not negatively (what they aren't).

### Phase 5 — Signal scan (cross-validation)

**Purpose:** Validate that the portrait pain points and opportunities identified from Reddit/forums are also present in other communities and formats. Guard against the research being an artefact of one community's particular framing.

**Primary method:** TikTok hashtag and content analysis (search for relevant hashtags, read video descriptions and caption content, note engagement metrics as signal of resonance). Supplement with YouTube content creator topic analysis where accessible.

**Note on YouTube comment sections:** YouTube comments are dynamically rendered and cannot be accessed directly. Substitute with: Reddit thread analysis within the same topic cluster, TikTok caption/description analysis, and forum posts referencing video content.

**Output requirements:**
- Engagement benchmarks for top content in the space (views, likes, comments as signal)
- Dominant content frame in the space (what is the current narrative? What is over-represented?)
- The gap between what content exists and what comments/community posts suggest is needed — this is the content opportunity signal

### Phase 6 — Language harvest

**Purpose:** Extract the exact words, phrases, and registers that the target population uses to describe their experience. This is not a summary. This is verbatim extraction organised by function.

**Output requirements (mandatory categories):**
1. Hot terms — exact phrases that recur across communities and carry high recognition value. These are the words to use in product copy verbatim.
2. Interior experience language — how they describe what it feels like from the inside.
3. Relationship-to-behaviour language — how they describe their pattern of behaviour.
4. Unspoken product brief — phrases that constitute an implicit product request.
5. Shame/negative language — words that existing products and communities use that break trust. These are constraints on copy.

**Quality gate:** Every phrase in categories 1–4 must have appeared in actual community posts. No paraphrase, no synthesis. The shame category may be inferred from what triggers defensive or dismissive responses in community threads.

### Phase 7 — Consumer insight statement

**Purpose:** Distill all research into a single, discipline-agnostic statement that makes the product direction feel inevitable. This is the sentence that aligns every team role without requiring them to read the full research.

**Format: observation → insight → implication (three-panel)**

- Observation: what is empirically true about the behaviour, stated without judgment
- Insight: why this is true — the underlying psychological or structural reason
- Implication: what this means a product must do

**The canonical insight statement:** A single sentence in quotation marks, in the user's register (not academic), that captures the insight and implication together. It should be short enough to repeat in a meeting. It should make someone who hasn't read the research say "yes, exactly."

**Quality test:** Read the insight statement to someone outside the project. If they say "that's obvious," the insight is surface-level. If they say "I've never thought of it that way but it's clearly true," the insight is good. If they say "I'm not sure that's true," the evidence base is insufficient.

### Phase 8 — White space analysis

**Format: side-by-side comparison per gap**

For each white space gap (typically 4–6):
- Left panel: existing solutions and their failure, with verbatim quotes from people who experienced that failure
- Right panel: what the gap demands — stated as a positive product requirement, not a critique of competitors
- The gap should be named as a design principle, not a feature

### Phase 9 — Analogous inspiration

**Purpose:** Find unrelated domains that have solved a structurally identical problem. Extract the transferable design principle.

**The IDEO framing:** "Analogous settings can help you isolate elements of an experience, interaction, or product, and then apply them to whatever design challenge you're working on."

**Method:** 
1. Name the core psychological function of the product (e.g., "redirect compulsive mental energy into a satisfying contained experience")
2. Ask: what other domains require this same function? Cast wide — rituals, games, material culture, clinical practice, industrial design, entertainment
3. For each analogy: what specific mechanism makes it work? What is the transferable principle?
4. Express as: "[domain] does X through [mechanism] → apply by [translation to our context]"

**Output requirements:** 5–7 analogies minimum. Each must have a concrete mechanism statement, not just a general observation. The translation must be specific enough to be actionable in a design session.

## Role packaging protocol

Role packages are the final section of every EISR document. They are tab-navigable, role-specific, and non-overlapping. Each role receives only what is directly actionable for their function.

**Framework basis:** Role package content is grounded in professional competency frameworks for each discipline. Do not invent what a role needs based on intuition — consult the relevant framework before populating a package. Key references: Mind the Product PM Competency Framework; UXmatters Five Competencies of UX Design; userfocus.co.uk 8 Competencies of UX; Odette Jansen UXR Competency Matrix; ProCopywriters 15-Competency UX Writing Framework; UX Content Collective Content Design 3.0; Reforge Growth Competency Model; CIM Global Professional Marketing Framework; Digital Analytics Association Competency Framework.

### Mandatory roles (always included)
- Product manager
- Engineer (mobile/frontend)
- UX designer
- Experience designer
- Content designer
- UX researcher
- Marketing / growth
- Data / analytics

### Additional roles (add when domain-specific)
- Industrial designer (hardware products)
- Clinical advisor (health/wellness products)
- Sound designer (audio-critical products)
- Legal/compliance (regulated industries)
- Partnerships (platform or B2B products)

### Role package structure (mandatory sections per role)

```
Role title
Primary question: what does this role need to know to do their job?
Role badge: domain label

Section 1 — What research directly provides this role
  Research-derived inputs: user segmentation, behavioural evidence, language, competitive 
  context. 3–5 items with verbatim support where relevant.

Section 2 — Behavioural and domain constraints
  Things the research establishes that constrain this role's work. Distinguished from 
  generic best practice — must be specific to this product and these users.

Section 3 — What research cannot provide — this role must investigate
  Explicit gaps: business model, technical feasibility, regulatory context, platform 
  constraints. These are downstream inputs the role must source independently. 
  Mark these clearly so roles know the boundary of what EISR supplies.

Section 4 — Analogous models relevant to this role's work (where applicable)
  1–2 specific analogies from Phase 9 that directly inform this role's decisions.

Next action: one concrete, specific next step
```

### What belongs in each role's package — framework-derived

**Product manager:** market opportunity, portrait-specific value propositions, product scope priorities (evidence-ranked), insight statement for PRD framing. *Must investigate independently:* business model and willingness-to-pay signal, technical feasibility of interaction requirements, PMF signal definition.

**Engineer:** behavioural constraints that generate technical requirements (impulsivity windows, interaction speed, sensory synchronisation, offline needs), portrait-specific system requirements (dark mode, notification constraints), data architecture constraints from research domain (sensitivity of user-generated content, instrumentation requirements). *Not supplied by research:* platform API capabilities, third-party dependencies, architecture decisions.

**UX designer:** mental models users bring to the product (derived from portrait psychology), critical flow requirements, edge cases and zero states, accessibility constraints generated by portrait needs, no-shame IA constraints. *Not supplied by research:* platform design system constraints, information architecture deliverables.

**Experience designer:** experience principles (unifying layer across all modes — must be derived from research before mode-level design begins), target emotional states by mode, transition design requirements between modes, sensory design requirements from portrait 2, emotional failure modes to design against. *Not supplied by research:* visual design language, sound design execution.

**Content designer:** language harvest (verbatim phrases for copy), hard-no list, voice direction, content system architecture requirements (content types inventory, reusable pattern identification, error/empty state taxonomy), accessibility and inclusive writing constraints. *Not supplied by research:* localisation scope, content management system constraints.

**UX researcher:** what is already established (do not re-research), research strategy ordered by decision dependency (not just method list), participant screening operationalisation (psychological portraits converted to observable screener questions), research ethics requirements when recruiting from sensitive communities, research repository structure. *Not supplied by research:* competitive intelligence on competitor retention/NPS, fieldwork logistics.

**Marketing / growth:** acquisition channels ranked by evidence density, portrait-specific propositions (not just generic positioning), positioning and anti-positioning, experimentation framework with testable hypotheses and go/no-go thresholds, retention and lifecycle arc. *Not supplied by research:* media buying rates, App Store optimisation category decisions, creator partnership commercial terms.

**Data / analytics:** research-derived hypotheses (specific and testable, not just metric targets), instrumentation requirements (events that must be built before launch to test each hypothesis), baseline-setting plan for a new product category with no benchmarks, portrait cohort segmentation schema, data privacy constraints from research domain. *Not supplied by research:* analytics platform selection, attribution modelling for community-driven growth.

### Non-conflation rule

No piece of information should appear in more than one role package. If the insight statement appears in the PM package, it should not be repeated in the UX package. If a technical constraint appears in the engineer package, it does not appear in the content designer package.

**Test:** If someone reads all role packages sequentially, they should feel they have received the full research — but in non-redundant portions. The packages tile together to form the whole, not repeat each other.

### The research boundary rule

Every role package must make explicit what EISR provides and what it does not. The "what research cannot provide" section in each package is not optional — it prevents roles from waiting for information that will never come from the research document, and stops them from treating research gaps as blockers rather than parallel workstreams.

## Document design standards

### Visual language
The EISR document is rendered as an interactive HTML widget with the following mandatory design principles:

- Serif display font for headings (Playfair Display or equivalent) — signals that this is editorial, considered, not a data dashboard
- Sans-serif body (DM Sans or system equivalent)
- Warm neutral background (#F9F7F3 or equivalent) — not clinical white, not dark
- Colour is used to encode portrait identity (each portrait has a consistent accent colour used throughout its section)
- Verbatim quotes use left-border accent in the portrait's colour + italic body text
- Language harvest phrases use pill/tag format — hot terms in accent colour, shame language in red-family
- Competitive landscape: card grid, each competitor has a "gap" field in italic accent text
- White space analysis: two-column side-by-side — existing (secondary background) / gap (white with accent border)
- Role packages: tab navigation, non-scrolling per tab, each tab self-contained
- Insight statement: dark inverted panel with serif italic quotation — the visual centrepiece of the document

### Verbatim evidence rules
- Every quote that appears in the document must be from an actual source
- Quotes are not paraphrased — they are reproduced as found, including grammatical imperfections
- Attribution format: (community name) — not individual usernames
- Quotes are not interchangeable between sections — a quote that appears in the community map does not reappear in the pain points section

## Common failure modes and how to avoid them

**Failure: portraits that are demographic categories in disguise**
Signs: portraits are described by age, income, or life stage rather than psychological function. Fix: rewrite each portrait's core psychology section to describe function, not category.

**Failure: research that confirms the brief rather than extending it**
Signs: every finding maps neatly to something the brief already stated. Fix: actively search for evidence that contradicts or complicates the portrait assumptions. Include it.

**Failure: language harvest that paraphrases**
Signs: the phrases in the harvest are grammatically clean and sound like marketing copy. Fix: return to raw community posts and extract verbatim. The grammatical imperfections are part of the signal.

**Failure: insight statement that describes the problem rather than explaining it**
Signs: the insight statement begins with "users feel..." or "people want...". Fix: the insight must state *why* — the structural or psychological reason — not just what. If you cannot explain why, the research depth is insufficient.

**Failure: role packages that repeat each other**
Signs: reading two role packages feels redundant. Fix: apply the non-conflation rule. Each package should answer a different question.

**Failure: role packages built on assumed needs rather than professional frameworks**
Signs: the package contains generic best-practice advice that any professional already knows, rather than research-specific inputs. Fix: consult the professional competency framework for the role before populating the package. Ask: what does this role need *from market research specifically* that they cannot derive themselves? Only that goes in the package.

**Failure: role packages that don't mark their own boundaries**
Signs: roles treat the package as a complete brief and wait for missing information rather than running parallel workstreams. Fix: every package must include an explicit "what research cannot provide — this role must investigate" section. This is not a weakness disclosure — it is a workflow directive.

**Failure: analogies that are superficial comparisons**
Signs: "this is like Netflix because both involve choice." Fix: the analogy must name a specific mechanism, not a surface similarity. What exactly does Netflix do that we should translate? Variable reward cadence? Autoplay removing decision friction? Be specific.

**Failure: white space that is just competitor criticism**
Signs: the "our gap" panel says "unlike Noom, we won't track calories." Fix: the gap panel must state a positive design requirement. What must we be, not what we won't be.

## Late-stage research integration protocol

EISR documents are not closed on first publication. New findings emerge after the main research is complete — from follow-up searches, from questions the human raises, from adjacent topics that surface unexpectedly. This protocol governs how those findings are integrated without degrading the document's structural integrity.

### The core principle: integration, not appendage

New findings are never added as a separate section or appendix unless they constitute a Type C or Type D finding (see below). Appending creates a two-tier document where late findings feel like afterthoughts. Integration propagates the finding through every phase it logically belongs to, treating it as if it had been found during the original research. The document should read as if the finding was always there.

### Step 1 — Classify the finding

Every late-stage finding falls into one of four types. Determine the type before doing anything else.

**Type A — New evidence for an existing entry**
A new verbatim quote, a newly discovered community, or additional data that supports something already in the document. Does not change any structural claim.

Integration: insert into the relevant phase card or section. Update any signal strength ratings if the new evidence changes the density. No structural changes. No new section.

Example: discovering a fifth verbatim quote that confirms Portrait 3's anticipatory behaviour pattern.

**Type B — New competitive entry or failed solution category**
A new product, approach, technique, or cultural phenomenon that belongs in the competitive landscape or failed solutions section. Does not reveal a new portrait type and does not change the insight statement.

Integration: multi-point propagation across phases. Add to Phase 3 (new failed solution card with verbatim), Phase 4 (new competitive landscape card), and Phase 8 (left panel of whichever white space gap it most sharply illuminates). Check Phase 9 — does it strengthen any existing analogy or suggest a new one? Update role packages only if the finding changes a specific role's constraints or opportunities in a material way.

Do not create a new section. Propagate through existing phases only.

Example: visual aversion / disgust-based appetite suppression tools. Belongs in Phase 3 as a new failed solution category, Phase 4 as a new competitor card, and Phase 8 as additional evidence in the "redirect not suppress" gap.

**Type C — New portrait**
Research reveals a psychological type that genuinely was not captured in the original portrait set. This is a meaningful structural intervention. Test before classifying: can this type's experience be substantially explained by one or two existing portraits in combination? If yes, it is not a new portrait — it is a nuance within existing portraits.

Integration: add the full portrait to Phase 2 (with complete portrait structure: core psychology, behavioural texture, situational framing, emotional logic, key feature). Add a corresponding community entry to Phase 1 with representative verbatim. Add any new language specific to this portrait to Phase 6. Review Phase 3 — does this portrait have distinct failed solutions not already covered? Review Phase 8 — does this portrait reveal a new white space gap? Update role packages where this portrait changes priorities. Update the insight statement if this portrait shifts the underlying psychological explanation.

**Type D — Paradigm-shifting finding**
A finding that materially changes the insight statement, restructures the white space conclusion, or reveals that a core assumption underpinning the research was wrong. This is rare.

Integration: flag to the human before integrating. State clearly what the existing claim is, what the new finding suggests, and what would change. Do not integrate silently. If confirmed, revise from Phase 7 onward: update the insight statement, cascade changes through Phase 8, Phase 9, and the role packages. Note the revision in the research log.

---

### Step 2 — Apply the propagation map

Use this map to ensure every phase that logically contains the finding is updated. Do not update only the most obvious phase.

| Finding type | Phase 1 | Phase 2 | Phase 3 | Phase 4 | Phase 5 | Phase 6 | Phase 7 | Phase 8 | Phase 9 | Role packages |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Type A | Maybe | Maybe | Maybe | — | — | Maybe | — | — | — | Only if priority-shifting |
| Type B | — | — | Always | Always | — | — | — | Always | Check | Only if constraint-shifting |
| Type C | Always | Always | Check | Check | — | Always | Check | Check | — | Always |
| Type D | — | — | Check | Check | — | — | Always | Always | Always | Always |

"Always" = must update. "Check" = review and update if relevant. "Maybe" = update if the finding is in this phase's scope. "—" = no update needed.

---

### Step 3 — Apply the non-redundancy rule to the new finding

Before integrating, check: does the new finding duplicate anything already in the document? If a failed solution category already covers the same psychological mechanism under a different name, the new finding is Type A evidence for that existing entry — not a new Type B entry. Merging is correct; duplicating is not.

Test: after integration, read the section where the finding lands alongside its neighbouring entries. Does the finding feel like it was always part of the research? If yes, integration is complete. If it feels like a footnote or caveat, the integration is too shallow.

---

### Step 4 — Update the research log

The research log is a brief metadata section at the end of the document (not a section in the main research body). It records:

- Date of the addition
- What was found and how (search terms, source, the human's question that prompted it)
- Type classification
- Which phases were updated

The research log makes the document's history visible without cluttering the research itself. It is also the mechanism that prevents the same territory from being searched redundantly in future sessions.

**Research log format:**

```
[Date] — [Finding summary in one sentence]
Type: [A/B/C/D]
Triggered by: [search / human question / adjacent research]
Phases updated: [list]
```

---

### The new-section exception

A new top-level section is only justified in one circumstance: when a batch of late-stage findings collectively constitute a new research dimension that spans multiple phases but doesn't fit cleanly into any of them. This is distinct from a Type D finding (which changes existing phases) — it is additive infrastructure.

The test: if you cannot describe the new section's content without using the phrase "things that didn't fit elsewhere," it is not a valid new section — it is filing avoidance. A valid new section has a single clear purpose that cannot be served by any existing phase.

In practice, this exception applies almost never. The nine-phase structure is designed to be comprehensive. If a finding genuinely has no home, the more likely explanation is that the phase taxonomy needs updating — which is a methodology revision, not a document revision.

### Integration decision tree (quick reference)

```
New finding arrives
│
├── Does it change the insight statement or core white space conclusion?
│   ├── Yes → Type D. Flag to human before integrating.
│   └── No → continue
│
├── Does it reveal a psychological portrait type not in the current set?
│   ├── Yes → Can it be explained by existing portraits in combination?
│   │   ├── Yes → Type A. Add as nuance within existing portrait.
│   │   └── No → Type C. Full portrait integration required.
│   └── No → continue
│
├── Is it a new product, technique, or cultural phenomenon in the space?
│   ├── Yes → Type B. Propagate through Phases 3, 4, 8. Check 9.
│   └── No → continue
│
└── Is it new evidence for something already in the document?
    └── Yes → Type A. Insert into relevant phase. Update signal strength if needed.
```

## Naming and professional classification

### Method name
**EISR — Experiential Insight Synthesis Research**

### Why this name

*Experiential* — because the entry point is the lived, interior experience of the problem, not its categorical label. The phenomenological brief is what makes this method structurally different from all other social listening approaches.

*Insight* — because the output is not data, not statistics, not a report of what people said. It is a synthesised understanding of *why* people behave as they do — the kind of insight that makes a product direction feel inevitable.

*Synthesis* — because the method aggregates across multiple community sources (Reddit, TikTok, forums, expert commentary) and synthesises them through a single psychological framework (the portrait set), rather than reporting each source separately.

*Research* — because this is rigorous, evidence-grounded, and traceable. Every claim must be backed by verbatim evidence. It is qualitative, not quantitative, but it is not impressionistic.

### Professional classification

EISR sits within the category of **qualitative discovery research** — specifically at the intersection of:

- Social listening synthesis (the data collection method)
- Ethnographic synthesis without fieldwork (the analytical approach — achieving thick description from public text rather than observation)
- IDEO-style analogous research (the creative translation layer)
- Brand strategy insight generation (the output format — insight statements, language harvest, competitive positioning)

**It is not:**
- Quantitative market research (no statistical claims, no representative sampling)
- UX usability research (no task testing, no interface evaluation)
- Academic research (not peer-reviewed, not designed for generalisability)
- Journalistic research (not aiming for comprehensive coverage, aiming for insight depth)

**The right moment for EISR:**
- Pre-product: discovery and direction-setting before a product concept exists
- Pre-brief: before writing a creative or product brief, to give it a human foundation
- Pre-positioning: before deciding how a product will talk about itself
- Post-quantitative: to explain *why* a quantitative finding is happening, when the numbers are clear but the reason is not

**EISR is not the right method for:**
- Market sizing (use quantitative syndicated research)
- Usability evaluation (use NN/G-style UX research)
- Concept validation at scale (use A/B testing or quantitative survey)
- Competitive pricing analysis (use primary research and public pricing data)

## Quick-start checklist

When beginning an EISR engagement, run through this before executing any research:

- [ ] Phenomenological brief received (interior narration, not problem label)
- [ ] Conventional problem framing received (domain, known community)
- [ ] Portrait scope clarified (all types equal, or anchor on contributor's experience)
- [ ] Portrait documents provided OR portrait generation protocol initiated
- [ ] Competitor names provided OR research will surface them
- [ ] Team role list confirmed
- [ ] Research depth confirmed (full / partial)
- [ ] Research scope clarified — is the contributor also in the target population? (If yes, their language is primary evidence, not just context)

When integrating a late-stage finding, run through this before touching the document:

- [ ] Finding classified (Type A / B / C / D)
- [ ] Propagation map checked — every phase that logically contains the finding identified
- [ ] Non-redundancy check done — does this duplicate an existing entry under a different name?
- [ ] If Type C or D — flagged to human before integrating
- [ ] All relevant phases updated
- [ ] Research log updated

**[June 2026] — Visual aversion / disgust-based appetite suppression identified as an uncovered failed-solution category**
Type: B
Triggered by: human question — "has our research covered any solution like watching videos that quench appetite to prevent eating, such as those videos that make delicious food look revolting or soaked in oil?"
Phases to update: Phase 3 (new failed solution card), Phase 4 (new competitive landscape card), Phase 8 (additional left-panel evidence for "redirect not suppress" gap)
Finding summary: A small category of tools and techniques exists that attempts to suppress appetite through visual disgust — revolting food images, colour-altered food photography apps (e.g. Eat Suppressor, App Store), and olfactory aversion conditioning. Clinical evidence does not support effectiveness at meaningful scale. Critically, clinical research shows that people with disordered eating patterns already exhibit significantly higher disgust sensitivity toward food images — meaning disgust-based tools risk reinforcing harm rather than helping in the population most likely to seek appetite suppression. This finding strengthens the "redirect not suppress" white space gap: all suppression approaches, including disgust-based ones, fail for the same structural reason — they fight the food-brain rather than giving it somewhere to go.
