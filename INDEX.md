# Method Bank — INDEX
## Investigation design reference for product and experience research

This index lists methods aligned to each problem type. Entries are drawn from the qualitative research literature (Creswell & Poth, 2018; Denzin & Lincoln, 2018; NNGroup research taxonomy) and UX practice. They are references — not prescriptions and not an exhaustive catalogue. A researcher identifies their problem type, reads the aligned methods, and selects a combination appropriate to their specific study constraints.

Methods marked *(documented)* have a profile in this bank. All others are candidates for documentation when applied.

---

## Problem types and aligned methods

### Type 1 — Interior experience problems

The phenomenon is what something feels like from inside. Private, often difficult to articulate, frequently shame-adjacent or socially suppressed. The gap between visible behaviour and interior experience is the research question.

*Examples:* food noise, anxiety in a use moment, grief after product loss, shame around a behaviour, desire in a purchase moment, creative flow states.

**Aligned methods:**

- **In-depth phenomenological interview** — guided first-person narration of the lived experience with researcher probing for depth, bodily language, and temporal texture. The most direct method for accessing interior structure when participants can articulate it. *(Moustakas, 1994; van Manen, 1990)*
- **Phenomenological netnography** — reading online community expression of the experience in its natural habitat. Unmediated by researcher presence. Suited when a rich community exists. *(Kozinets, 2020)* → `phenomenological-netnography.md` *(documented)*
- **ESM / Ecological Momentary Assessment** — real-time capture of the experience as it occurs across daily life via prompted self-report. Suited when temporal structure matters. *(Myin-Germeys et al., 2009)* → `esm-diary-study.md` *(documented)*
- **Cultural probe** — participant-directed documentation kit deployed before primary study. Participants photograph, voice-record, or complete sentence prompts about their experience in their own register, without researcher categories imposed. *(Gaver, Dunne & Pacenti, 1999)*
- **Diary study** — extended self-report log kept over days or weeks. Suited for experiences with irregular frequency where ESM signal scheduling is impractical. *(NNGroup)*
- **Narrative inquiry** — structured collection and analysis of personal stories about the experience. Researchers re-story participants' accounts to surface meaning, temporal structure, and identity relationships. *(Creswell & Poth, 2018)*
- **Hermeneutic phenomenological interview** — interpretation-focused variant of phenomenological interview; researcher and participant collaboratively examine texts, objects, or written accounts of experience. *(van Manen, 1990)*
- **Autoethnography** — researcher uses their own embodied experience of the phenomenon as primary data, situated within a broader cultural or social analysis. Suited when the researcher shares the experience being studied. *(Ellis, Adams & Bochner, 2011)*
- **Retrospective interview** — depth probe and validity check using prior data as memory scaffold. → `retrospective-interview.md` *(documented)*

### Type 2 — Behavioural pattern problems

The question is what people actually do, when, and in what sequence. Often diverges significantly from what people report. Requires capturing behaviour rather than accounts of behaviour.

*Examples:* navigation patterns, task abandonment, workarounds, unconscious usage sequences, conversion or churn precursors, habitual use patterns.

**Aligned methods:**

- **Contextual inquiry** — observation and interview combined in the participant's natural work or use environment. Reveals underlying work structure, workarounds, and invisible behaviours. *(Holtzblatt & Beyer, 1998; NNGroup)*
- **Field study / ethnographic observation** — extended observation in the natural setting with varying degrees of researcher participation. Suited for understanding behaviour within its social and environmental context. *(Fetterman, 2019; NNGroup)*
- **Digital trace / clickstream analytics** — passive capture of behavioural sequences at scale within an existing product. Temporal precision and population coverage no observation study can match. *(NNGroup)*
- **Diary study with structured logging** — participants log specific behavioural events as they occur, using a structured template. Suited where behaviour spans multiple sessions or days and cannot be directly observed. *(NNGroup)*
- **ESM** — real-time signal-based capture suited for behaviours with temporal structure. → `esm-diary-study.md` *(documented)*
- **Task analysis** — systematic breakdown of how users perform a specific task: goals, steps, sequence, information needs. Often combines interview and contextual inquiry. *(NNGroup)*
- **Think-aloud protocol** — participant narrates their actions and reasoning while performing a task. Behavioural and attitudinal data simultaneously. *(NNGroup)*
- **Eye-tracking** — captures visual attention patterns during product interaction. Reveals what is seen, ignored, or fixated on. *(NNGroup)*
- **Usability testing (moderated or unmoderated)** — structured task completion with observation, suited for identifying specific friction points in an existing interface. *(NNGroup)*
- **A/B testing** — controlled deployment of design variants to measure behavioural difference at scale. Suited post-prototype. *(NNGroup)*
- **Retrospective interview with behavioural scaffold** — uses specific observed behaviours or usage data anomalies as interview prompts. → `retrospective-interview.md` *(documented)*

### Type 3 — Decision and sense-making problems

The question is how people make choices, what factors they weigh, and how they construct meaning when facing options or information. Stated preferences and actual decision processes routinely diverge.

*Examples:* pricing tier selection, trust signal evaluation, data-sharing consent decisions, upgrade decisions, product comparison, risk assessment.

**Aligned methods:**

- **Think-aloud protocol during live decision** — participant narrates reasoning in real time while making a real or simulated decision. Captures factors invisible to retrospective account. *(NNGroup)*
- **Cultural probe** — pre-study kit surfacing the language and frames people already use around a decision domain before researcher categories are introduced.*
- **Retrospective interview with decision scaffold** — structured walkthrough of a past decision step by step, using a specific protocol to surface implicit factors. → `retrospective-interview.md` *(documented)*
- **Concept testing** — attitudinal method collecting early reactions to a product idea before it exists. Identifies whether users would want or need the product. *(NNGroup)*
- **Participatory design** — participants co-create design solutions, revealing their mental models and decision priorities through the choices they make. *(NNGroup)*
- **Card sorting** — participants group items according to their own logic, revealing the cognitive schema underlying their understanding of a domain. *(NNGroup)*
- **Conjoint analysis** — statistical technique presenting participants with forced trade-off choices to reveal which factors actually drive decisions. Distinguishes stated from revealed preference at scale. *(NNGroup)*
- **Desirability study** — participants select words describing their reaction to a design; reveals aesthetic and affective schema shaping evaluation. *(NNGroup)*
- **Discourse analysis** — analysis of the language people use when describing or justifying decisions. Reveals framing, identity claims, and the social norms operating in decision contexts. *(Denzin & Lincoln, 2018)*
- **Focus group** — less suited for individual decision reconstruction but useful for mapping the shared norms, language, and social performance around a decision domain. *(NNGroup)*

### Type 4 — Social and contextual problems

The question is how context, environment, and social dynamics shape the experience or behaviour — how the same person acts or feels differently depending on who is present, where they are, or what social norms are operating.

*Examples:* social presence effects on eating, open office effects on focus, peer comparison in fitness apps, platform norm effects on self-disclosure, family context in financial decisions, how community membership shapes product use.

**Aligned methods:**

- **Ethnography / extended field study** — immersive observation within a specific community or context over an extended period. Studies behaviour as embedded in social and cultural systems. *(Creswell & Poth, 2018)*
- **Contextual inquiry across multiple settings** — systematic comparison of behaviour and experience in different contexts within the same participant. The contrast between settings is the primary data. *(Holtzblatt & Beyer, 1998)*
- **ESM with context logging** — moment-to-moment capture across contexts, participants, and days. Context fields (location, social presence, activity) are primary analysis dimensions. → `esm-diary-study.md` *(documented)*
- **Netnography** — studies online communities as cultural systems. Suited when the social context of interest is an online community and its norms, language, and shared meaning-making. → `phenomenological-netnography.md` *(documented)*
- **Conversation analysis** — detailed analysis of naturally-occurring interaction to surface the rules and norms governing how people communicate in a specific social setting. *(Schegloff; Drew & Heritage, 1992)*
- **Discourse analysis** — examines how language use reflects and reproduces social norms, power relations, and cultural frames. *(Denzin & Lincoln, 2018)*
- **Participatory action research** — researcher and community members collaborate as co-investigators to understand a shared social problem and generate action. Suited when the research question has a change or justice dimension. *(Creswell & Poth, 2018)*
- **Photovoice / Videovoice** — participants document their social context through photographs or video, then discuss what they captured. Surfaces lived experience of context without requiring verbal articulation. *(Wang & Burris, 1997)*
- **Diary study with social context tagging** — participants log experiences with structured social context fields. Suited where contextual inquiry is not feasible. *(NNGroup)*
- **Retrospective interview focused on contextual contrast** — probes why context matters, using specific observed contextual contrasts as scaffolds. → `retrospective-interview.md` *(documented)*

### Type 5 — Longitudinal and change problems

The question is how something evolves over time — across a product adoption curve, a life transition, or in response to a design intervention. Single-point research cannot answer these questions.

*Examples:* mental model shift over first 30 days, habit formation or breakdown, attitude change after a major update, life event effects on product relationship, how a community's norms change over time.

**Aligned methods:**

- **Longitudinal diary study with check-in interviews** — tracks change as it happens across weeks or months. Periodic short check-in interviews maintain engagement and allow probing before memories fade.
- **Before-and-after interview pair** — bookend accounts of a change from the participant's own perspective. Only meaningful when a documented baseline interview exists.
- **ESM nested within a longitudinal window** — for phases where moment-to-moment texture matters within a longer change arc. → `esm-diary-study.md` *(documented)*
- **Baseline survey or structured interview** — establishes the pre-change state systematically. Without a documented baseline, change cannot be measured.
- **Narrative inquiry** — collects and re-stories personal accounts of change over time, surfacing how participants make sense of transitions and turning points. *(Creswell & Poth, 2018)*
- **Case study** — in-depth investigation of change within a bounded real-world context (a team, a product, an organisation). Suited when the change is complex, context-dependent, and involves multiple actors or forces. *(Yin, 2018)*
- **Longitudinal netnography** — reading community expression across an extended time period to track how shared language, norms, and meaning around a phenomenon evolve. *(Kozinets, 2020)*
- **Life history / biographical interview** — extended interview covering the participant's full history with a product category or experience domain. Suited for understanding how long-term context shapes present behaviour. *(Denzin & Lincoln, 2018)*
- **Grounded theory** — suited for building a substantive theory of a change process from the ground up. Particularly appropriate when no prior theory exists for how a specific transition unfolds. *(Glaser & Strauss, 1967; Corbin & Strauss, 2015)*

## Combination principles

**No study relies on a single method.** Every method has structural blindspots. Pairing logic in each bank profile names what the method cannot do.

**Primary/secondary logic applies.** Select a primary method that determines the data type and sampling approach. Secondary methods fill its blindspots. Two methods of equal weight pulling in different directions is a design failure.

**Round 1 phenomenological inquiry precedes Round 2 measurement for all Type 1 studies.** The phenomenological inquiry process is in `DESIGN_INVESTIGATION_SKILL.md`. Round 2 instruments are designed from the Round 1 composite summary — not from prior market research assumptions.

**Prior research changes what Round 1 needs to do.** With substantial prior market research, Round 1 refines and deepens. Without it, Round 1 is more broadly exploratory. If no prior market research exists, run EISR before this stage.

## Contribution protocol

A method enters the bank when applied in a project and documented with enough operational detail that a researcher on a different project could follow it without having participated.

**Minimum standard for a new profile:** When to use it · What it cannot do · What it pairs with · Decision trigger · At least one real application described.

**How profiles update:** New applications add examples. New limitations or pairing logic are appended. Profiles accumulate — they are not rewritten.

**What stays out:** Methods not yet applied in a closely adjacent project. Entries in this index are candidates — they enter the bank only when applied and documented.

## Production calibration references — apply during Stage 4+5

These are not method profiles. They are calibration tools for practitioners producing the three terminal documents (Brand System, Product Specification, Strategic Direction). They answer "what does correct look like?" for two production decisions where vagueness is the most common failure mode.

---

### HMW statement scoping

**What it calibrates:** whether a How Might We statement is correctly scoped — broad enough to invite multiple concept directions, narrow enough to rule out irrelevant ones.

**The failure modes and their signals:**

| Type | Signal | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Too broad | Any concept direction can answer it | "How might we improve the food noise experience?" |
| Too narrow | Implies a specific solution | "How might we add a 30-second voice note to the craving capture flow?" |
| Correct scope | 3 distinct directions could answer it, but irrelevant ones cannot | "How might we give the energy of a craving somewhere to go without requiring the person to eat or suppress?" |

**The three-direction test:** before finalising a HMW statement, sketch three genuinely different directions that answer it. If you cannot produce three, the statement is too narrow. If any direction in the product space could answer it, the statement is too broad.

**Scope signals to watch for:**
- Contains a solution noun (voice note, notification, session) → too narrow, remove the noun
- Contains only the problem domain with no constraint ("food noise", "the experience") → too broad, add one constraint from the Stage 3 design principles
- Contains a verb that implies a specific interaction (add, build, create, send) → too narrow, replace with an outcome verb (give, enable, allow, make possible)

---

### Concept direction distinctness

**What it calibrates:** whether three concept directions are genuinely distinct or are surface treatment variations of the same direction. The most common failure mode in Section 02 of the Strategic Direction.

**The test:** state each direction's position on the key Stage 3 tensions. If two directions take the same position on all key tensions and differ only in execution style, they are not distinct — they are one direction with two visual treatments.

**The difference:**

*Surface treatment variation (not distinct):*
- Direction A: "The product feels like a calm companion — ambient, soft, present"
- Direction B: "The product feels like a focused tool — minimal, direct, efficient"
Both take the same position on all Stage 3 tensions. They differ in aesthetic register only. Collapse to one direction and develop a genuinely different third.

*Genuinely distinct directions:*
- Direction A: Positions food noise as a signal worth listening to — the product amplifies and names it before redirecting. Leans into the ego-syntonic tension: the craving has something to say.
- Direction B: Positions food noise as an energy state the body is in — the product provides a physical outlet that receives the energy without interpretation. Leans into the bodily-onset finding: the body moved before the mind registered it.
- Direction C: Positions food noise as a social experience disguised as a private one — the product makes the witnessed dimension explicit. Leans into the privacy/witnessing tension.

Each takes a different position on a different Stage 3 tension. Each implies different interaction design, different copy register, and different product architecture. A team selecting between them is making a real product decision — not choosing an aesthetic.

**Practical check:** for each direction, ask "which Stage 3 tension does this lean into, and how?" If the answer is the same for two directions, they are not distinct.
