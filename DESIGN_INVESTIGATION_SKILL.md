# Design How to Investigate It — SKILL

**Phase position:** Follows Frame A Problem (the stage where the product problem is defined, research reframed, and constraints established). Precedes Stage 3 — Produce Structured Insight (the stage where fieldwork findings are synthesised into product decisions).
**Role perspective:** Experience design owns research questions and phenomenological inquiry. UX owns plan, screener, and method procedure.
**Output:** Two working documents — generative research questions, and protocol + plan + screener.
**Method selection:** Consult `/method-bank/INDEX.md` before beginning. Identify your problem type and route to the recommended method combination. This skill specifies how to produce the documents and what is immutable across all studies. The bank specifies which methods to use. When a study requires a method listed as undocumented in the INDEX, use the INDEX entry as a starting point, apply it in the study, then document it as a bank profile after the study completes — following the contribution protocol in the INDEX.

**What Round 1 and Round 2 mean:** Investigation at this stage runs in two rounds. Round 1 is qualitative discovery — understanding what the phenomenon is, how it is experienced, what prior research did and did not capture. Round 2 is measurement — testing specific findings from Round 1 in real-time, in-context, with recruited participants. Round 2 instruments (ESM prompts, interview guides, screeners) are designed from the Round 1 composite summary. This sequencing is what keeps Round 2 from measuring a researcher construct rather than the phenomenon itself.

---

## Core production principle

Before writing any section, ask: who reads this, what decision do they need to make, and what language do they use for that decision?

**Experience designer reads:** what to investigate and why this method over alternatives.
**UX researcher reads:** how to execute the study operationally, step by step.
**Prototyper reads:** what decisions this research will open — a range, not a verdict.
**PM reads:** what cannot be decided without this research, and what changes if findings differ from expectations.

Write each section to its reader. When a section serves multiple readers, name the primary reader and write to them.

## Immutable requirement — phenomenological inquiry in Round 1

*Applies to: Type 1 interior experience problems. For Type 2–5 problems, consult the method bank INDEX — this requirement does not apply.*

For any Type 1 study, before any Round 2 measurement study runs, a phenomenological inquiry must be conducted. This is not a method — it is an analytical process that runs through whatever data source the study collects: netnographic community data, interview transcripts, diary entries. The method is chosen from the bank. The inquiry process below is invariant.

Phenomenological inquiry means reading qualitative data to understand the structure of an experience from the inside — what it is, how it feels, what is always present regardless of who is experiencing it. It is distinct from thematic analysis (which identifies recurring patterns) because it asks what is essential to the experience, not just what is common.

**Why this is immutable:** Round 2 studies impose a measurement framework on the phenomenon. If that framework is derived from prior market research assumptions rather than from the lived experience itself, the study measures a researcher construct rather than the phenomenon. The phenomenological inquiry ensures that the framework for Round 2 comes from the data.

**Two conditions that change how the process runs:**
- *If no prior market research exists:* Step 4 (prior research comparison marking) still runs, but all units default to "No prior equivalent" — the comparison is against an empty base. The process is more exploratory; saturation in condition 2 of the stopping rule becomes the primary termination criterion.
- *If the phenomenon has no bodily dimension:* Step 3 (flag embodied units) is skipped. Applicable to cognitive or linguistic phenomena where somatic experience is not the research question.

### The phenomenological inquiry process

**Before each session — bracketing**

Write a methodological note listing the prior research assumptions you are actively carrying into this session. Bracketing means naming your preconceptions explicitly — not eliminating them, but making them visible so you can notice when the data contradicts or extends them. For studies with prior market research, this means listing which portrait types, insight statement claims, and product directions you currently hold. These become the horizon against which new findings produce meaning.

**Step 1 — Gestalt read**
Read the whole data unit (thread, transcript, diary period) before extracting anything. Attend to overall shape — tone shifts, what the person returns to, what feels most charged. A unit means something different in the context of the whole than it does in isolation. This step cannot be skipped.

*Output: a sense of the whole before any extraction begins.*

**Step 2 — Delineate units of meaning**
Go through the data identifying discrete units — a phrase, a sentence, a passage that constitutes a distinct moment of meaning. A unit of meaning is the smallest piece of data that says something complete and distinct on its own. When in doubt whether two adjacent passages are one unit or two, keep them separate. Tag each unit with its coverage dimension — which dimension from the Document 02 data selection audit this unit speaks to. Coverage dimensions are defined per study in Document 02, Section: data selection.

*Output: a list of meaning units with coverage dimension tags.*

**Step 3 — Flag embodied units**
Mark any unit describing physical sensation, involuntary body movement, or somatic experience. These are methodologically prioritised — they provide direct access to the bodily layer of the experience that other analytical approaches routinely miss.

*Output: an embodied sub-corpus for priority analysis.*

**Step 4 — Prior research comparison marking**
For each unit mark one of four states: Extends prior research (adds to what was known) / Complicates prior research (introduces tension with a prior finding) / No prior equivalent (territory prior research did not reach) / May revise prior finding (suggests a prior claim needs to be updated). Units marked No prior equivalent are flagged immediately — they constitute the primary analytical output of Round 1. Accumulate them actively.

*Output: a sorted corpus. The "No prior equivalent" pile is what this inquiry exists to produce.*

**Step 5 — Eliminate redundancies**
Consolidate units with the same literal content across sessions. Note: frequency and distribution are not redundant even when content is the same. A unit appearing across multiple communities or participants with increasing emotional intensity is stronger evidence than one appearing once.

*Output: a consolidated unit list with frequency and source distribution noted.*

**Step 6 — Cluster into themes**
Group units sharing a common quality. Do not force clustering. Units that resist grouping are methodologically significant — they often point to structures the prior framework cannot accommodate. Note resistance explicitly in the analytical memo.

*Output: a theme map with outliers explicitly noted.*

**Step 7 — Cross-case comparison**
Ask which themes appear across all data sources — all communities, all participants, all contexts. Themes appearing everywhere are candidates for the shared core the research questions are asking about. Source-specific themes reveal how context shapes the experience.

*Output: a split between shared patterns and source-specific ones.*

**Step 8 — Validity checking**
Return phenomenological descriptions to participants or communities. Ask: does this describe what it feels like? Recognition confirms. Corrections are equally valuable — they surface what the description missed. In netnographic settings: post as a genuine community member with transparent research purpose. In interview settings: present the description at the end of a follow-up session.

*Output: community-validated or participant-corrected descriptions.*

**Step 9 — Composite summary**
Write a description of what the phenomenon looks like from the data — not from the prior research framework. At the end, explicitly compare to the prior research: what is confirmed, what is extended, what needs revision. This comparison is the handoff to Round 2 design.

*Output: the Round 1 primary deliverable. Round 2 instruments are not designed until this exists.*

### Memoing system — four types, maintained throughout

A memo in qualitative research is a brief written note the researcher makes during or after data collection to record what they observed, what they think it means, and what they need to do next. The memo system separates four types of thinking that are easy to conflate. It runs in parallel with all nine steps.

- **ON (Observational):** What happened — specific data noted as significant. Source, timestamp, context. Description only, no interpretation.
- **TN (Theoretical):** Attempts to derive meaning — what this data suggests about the structure of the experience. Prior research comparison tag attached.
- **MN (Methodological):** The session's bracketing list — active prior research assumptions named at session start. Renewed at the start of each new data source. This is the bracketing discipline made operational.
- **AM (Analytical):** End-of-session summary — what is emerging, what the data is resisting, what has no prior equivalent, what coverage gaps remain. The AM is the mechanism that tracks outstanding gaps between sessions and drives the stopping rule.

### Stopping rule

Data collection closes when all three conditions are met in sequence:

1. All primary coverage dimensions show sufficient variation (minimum 4–5 instances per dimension covering meaningfully different cases). *Coverage dimensions are defined per study in Document 02, Section: data selection — primary, secondary, and quality-check tiers specified there.*
2. Experiential saturation: three consecutive sessions with no new "No prior equivalent" units and no existing clusters gaining new members.
3. Gaps flagged in prior AMs are filled.

Secondary dimensions require sufficient variation, not saturation. Quality-check dimensions require balance only.

## Document 01 — Research questions

**Owner:** Experience design
**Reader:** Product team and UX researcher

**What this document is:** A set of open questions that direct fieldwork toward territory the team has genuine uncertainty about. A research question at this stage is not a hypothesis (a belief to be tested), not a brief (a specification of what to build), and not a survey question (designed to collect responses at scale). Its function is to keep inquiry genuinely open — to ensure that fieldwork can produce findings the team did not anticipate, not only confirm what was already believed. The document is written before fieldwork begins and used throughout Round 1 to keep analysis oriented toward discovery rather than confirmation.

### What makes a research question work

A research question is open if someone could answer it in a way that surprises you. If the question contains its own analytical framework as a premise, it is a hypothesis disguised as a question.

**Test:** Remove everything after the question mark. Does what remains contain analytical categories or preset options? If yes, rewrite.

Failure: *"What is the temporal and rhythmic structure of food noise?"* — contains the analytical frame.
Working: *"What happens from the moment a craving begins to when it ends or passes?"* — genuinely open.

### Card structure — four elements

**The question** — plain language, non-leading, no analytical categories, no preset options.

**Three side-by-side fields with directional arrows:**
- *What this asks about:* Plain description of the territory this question covers. One or two sentences. No frameworks.
- *What we don't know yet:* The specific gap between prior research and this question — what prior research captured and what it missed. This field makes the purpose of the question explicit: it names the exact uncertainty that makes asking it worthwhile.
- *What this opens:* A list of decisions, processes, and prototype choices the finding will inform. Not a verdict. Not a product feature name — the product does not exist yet. Written in the language of the roles who act on it: session architecture, interaction model, copy register, prototype scope. The reader selects what is relevant to where they are.

**Prior research comparison marker** — marks the relationship between this question and what prior market research already found. Four states: Extends (this question builds on a prior finding) / May complicate (this question introduces tension with a prior finding) / No prior equivalent (this question covers territory prior research did not reach) / May revise (this question may change a foundational prior claim). The marker exists so findings can be compared to the prior evidence base explicitly — not to validate the prior research but to build on it with precision.

**Production trigger** — names one specific role and one specific decision that changes depending on what this question finds. A production trigger is a short directive: "Role X will need to decide Y." It makes the stakes of the question concrete for the team member who acts on the finding.

### What does not appear in Document 01

Analytical taxonomy in the questions. Options embedded in the question as prompts. Product feature names in "What this opens." Philosophical citations. Single-outcome verdicts.

## Document 02 — Protocol + plan + screener

**Owner:** Experience design (protocol and method rationale) + UX (plan, screener, method procedure)
**Reader:** UX researcher executing the study

**What this document is:** The operational brief for running the study. The protocol states which methods will be used, in what order, and why these methods over alternatives — it is the methodological rationale a researcher reads before fieldwork begins. The plan states who will be recruited, how many, and according to what sampling strategy. The screener is the instrument used to determine whether a candidate qualifies for the study before they are recruited. Together these three sections give the researcher everything needed to run the study without further instruction.

*This protocol executes the data collection that feeds the phenomenological inquiry process defined in the skill above. For Type 1 studies, Round 2 instruments are designed from the Round 1 composite summary produced by that inquiry — not from prior market research assumptions.*

### Section: data selection — maximum variation coverage

Maximum variation means deliberately seeking out data that represents the widest possible range of the phenomenon — not a convenient or homogeneous sample. Coverage means ensuring that range is systematically checked rather than accidentally achieved. Together, maximum variation coverage is a data selection strategy that asks: before I stop collecting data, have I seen the full range of how this phenomenon presents itself?

Derive coverage dimensions from the research questions. For each question ask: what range of data would this question require to produce a credible finding? That range is a coverage dimension.

**Weighting is mandatory:**
- *Primary dimensions* — address foundational questions about what the phenomenon is. Must reach saturation before secondary dimensions are evaluated.
- *Secondary dimensions* — address contextual and surrounding questions. Sufficient variation required, not saturation.
- *Quality-check dimensions* — ensure methodological balance in the corpus. Do not independently surface new experiential content.

**Two standing rules applicable to any study using this methodology:**
1. *Trigger condition is always a primary dimension* — the specific circumstances immediately before the experience began. What was the person doing, feeling, or encountering at the moment of onset? This dimension is consistently underinvestigated in prior market research and is the most likely source of findings the prior research did not reach.
2. *Communicative function is always a quality-check dimension* — different communicative styles (confession, advice-seeking, identity claim, validation-seeking) package the same experiential content differently for an audience. They do not independently surface different experiential structures. This dimension prevents corpus skew in tone and framing but is not a substantive coverage dimension.

### Section: method procedure

Written as numbered steps. Each step names what to do, the standard or guideline it follows, and where it departs from standard — flagged explicitly with rationale. Departures from standard are methodological innovations: name them, do not hide them.

### Section: method rationale

Three-column comparison table. Centre column: the specific research challenge this study faces — not a generic problem statement, but what this problem specifically costs in this study. Right column: how the chosen method addresses this challenge. Left column: one or more alternative methods with specific reasons they are less well-suited given these constraints. "Less well-suited given these constraints" not "wrong."

### Section: participant criteria and screener

**Sampling strategy must be named explicitly.** Maximum variation purposive sampling means selecting participants deliberately to represent the widest range of the phenomenon — not randomly, not conveniently, but purposively to cover variation. Criterion sampling means every participant must meet a minimum threshold for the phenomenon to be present at all. Combined: all participants meet the minimum criterion, and together they represent maximum variation across relevant dimensions.

State quotas per subgroup — minimum numbers of participants representing each portrait type or relevant variation category — with the research question each quota serves.

Tie each screener question to the quota it fills and state pass/fail criteria explicitly.

**Open-field articulation question** — one open-text question asking participants to describe the experience in their own words. Purpose: assessing whether participants can access and describe interior experience, not just behaviour. Responses using interior, sensory, or emotional language indicate higher suitability for phenomenological data requirements. Flag as non-standard with stated rationale — standard screeners use closed items only.

**Foil question** — one question that includes a small number of unrelated, implausible items alongside relevant ones. Purpose: identifying inattentive respondents who select options indiscriminately. Flag it explicitly as a foil. State the threshold for flagging (e.g. selecting 5 or more options including the unrelated items).

## Format standards

Role tags at the top of each document are functional — they tell the reader whether the document is for them. Section labels orient; content explains. Footnotes cite sources, methodology standards, and named departures only.

## Common failure modes

**Writing for the wrong reader.** Check the role line before writing each section.

**Questions that contain their own answers.** Apply the test: remove everything after the question mark.

**"What this opens" as a verdict.** A list opens decision space. A single framed outcome closes it.

**Skipping the gestalt read.** Extracting units from posts or transcripts without reading the whole first produces decontextualised data. The gestalt step is non-negotiable.

**Coverage without weighting.** Primary dimensions must reach saturation before secondary dimensions are evaluated. Trigger condition is always primary. Communicative function is always a quality check.

**Missing stopping rule.** The AM memo is what makes the stopping rule operational. Without end-of-session memos, gap tracking fails and data collection has no principled endpoint.

**Round 2 designed before Round 1 composite summary exists.** ESM prompts, interview guides, and screeners designed from prior market research assumptions rather than phenomenological findings measure researcher constructs, not the phenomenon.
